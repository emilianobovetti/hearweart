Hear We Art

empowering through creativity

Hear We Art is a project specifically designed for the hearing and visually impaired, including children from 4 to 18 years old. Our aim is to enrich children skills and abilities in practical art making. Our strong believe is that the arts have the capacity to develop social equity, interdependence and empowerment. It is therefore important that the arts are available to as many young people as possible.

As The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities and Convention on the Rights of the Child says: “access to the arts is a fundamental right for all children and young people, the child has the right to participate freely in cultural life and have equal opportunities for cultural, artistic,recreational and leisure activities.”

    How does Hear We Art work?

  • By improving the knowledge of the teachers and integrating locals with the right vocational skills, give the school the ability to run future workshops and training on their own, in future.
  • Using recycled materials such as newspaper, plastic lids, used containers, cardboard therefore learning about eco-friendly art and sustainability.
  • Full, hands-on induction for the teachers and pupils to GIMP Graphic Design software, as specifically requested by the school board.
  • This will begin a process of enhancement of the children’s career and employment prospects in Graphic Design. (To be developed in 2018)
  • Boost the children’s confidence, self-esteem and sense of commitment to their studies overall, and to the school.
  • Have lots of fun and enjoyment. We all know how all kids the world over love technology and ‘getting their hands dirty’!
  • Collaborate with people form different cultures and backgrounds. Exchange their creative viewpoint with artists from Rome and London to create an international inspiring collaboration.

Senkadagala School of Deaf and Blind

The Hear We Art workshops has been developed specifically for Senkadagala school for Deaf and Blind children. The school is comprises 87 deafand 5 blind students and was founded in 1962, by a blind teacher, Rienzie Alagiyawanna. It is a public school which gives the children free schooling and free access and to healthcare.

The school, and therefore the children have to follow the Sri Lankan national curriculum, which includes reading and writing, as well as learning deaf language, English and international deaf language. Most of the children struggle to keep-up with theoretical subjects, however, many of them are very talented in art and creative subjects, which unfortunately have no place in the normal curriculum. The children usually leave school with poor academic results because the curriculum is not adapted to their disabilities consequently few are able to get go on to further education. And with little, if any, vocational training, the great majority have to get unskilled, very poorly paid work, if they find anything at all.

To meet help the children develop and capitalize on their natural creative abilities, the school board have decided to introduce a few afternoon classes led by volunteer tutors in subjects such as computer, photography, graphic design, woodcarving and dress making. But often the teachers have quite limited knowledge and experience of teaching these subjects.
